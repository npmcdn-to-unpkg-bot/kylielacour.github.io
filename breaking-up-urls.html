Breaking Up URLS

Ever wonder how to break long URLs at the end of a line when doing a book layout? You got tons of URLs in the bibliography section and they are quite long! Tracking and kerning helps a lot. But what if they’re too long for tracking? With the help of The Chicago Manual of Style, learn how to divide a long URL or website link properly.

1 BASIC RULE:

The basic rule is as follows: do not use a hyphen to break the URL at the end of a line. This means that we should never break the line by hyphenating it whether manually or not. Most URLs are hyperlinked and “accidentally” adding another character to it will not yield to the intended site. So how do we break it then?

The Chicago Manual of Style’s rule 14.12—URLs or DOIs and line breaks gives us a clear description.

In a printed work, if a URL or DOI has to be broken at the end of a line, the break should be made after a colon or a double slash (//); before a single slash (/), a tilde (~), a period, a comma, a hyphen, an underline (_), a question mark, a number sign, or a percent symbol; or before or after an equals sign or an ampersand. Such breaks help to signal that the URL or DOI has been carried over to the next line. A hyphen should never be added to a URL or DOI to denote a line break, nor should a hyphen that is part of a URL or DOI appear at the end of a line.

2 Examples:

http://grooveshark.com/#!
/playlist/Layout+YeaH/92618207

http://nowwhatsmyname.wordpress.com/wp-admin/edit.php?post_type
=page

3 MORE TIPS

Always remember to use a soft return (shift + enter) when breaking the URL since it is one long address in its nature. That is, if kerning and tracking won’t do the trick.

Also, to make things easier, sentences that include an e-mail address or a URL should be punctuated normally. Place punctuation (a period or a question mark) after a URL, just in case the author forgot to. Readers usually assume that any punctuation at the end of an e-mail address or URL belongs to the sentence.

4 CHEAT SHEET

after:

colon (:)
double slash (//)

before:

single slash (/)
a tilde (~)
period (.)
comma (,)
hyphen (-)
underline (_)
question mark (?)
number sign (#)
percent symbol (%)

before or after:

equals sign (=)
ampersand (&)
